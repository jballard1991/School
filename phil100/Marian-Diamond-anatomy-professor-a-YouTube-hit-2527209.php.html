Marian Diamond - anatomy professor a YouTube hit

SUNDAY PROFILE / Marian Diamond

Marian Diamond - anatomy professor a YouTube hit

Each fall, Marian Diamond walks into UC Berkeley's Wheeler Auditorium holding a round hatbox, a gift from the staff of Lawrence Hall of Science, where she used to serve as director.

The 84-year-old professor stands on the stage dressed in an elegant blazer and vibrantly colored scarf, her white hair drawn back in a French twist. She pulls on rubber gloves and removes a chunk of coiled gray matter from a plastic container inside the hatbox.

What happens next has been watched nearly a half-million times on the university's YouTube channel, where Diamond's introductory anatomy lecture is the most viewed video.

"The brain is the most complex mass of protoplasm on this earth," Diamond says proudly, holding it in one hand. "It only weighs 3 pounds, but it has the capacity to conceive of a universe a billion light-years across. Isn't that phenomenal?"

Forget about PowerPoint presentations. After decades in the classroom, Diamond lectures the old-fashioned way - by writing on a blackboard. She expects her students to take notes with pen and paper so they can better absorb the information.

Diamond's discoveries in neuroscience have captured international attention and revolutionized how scientists view the brain's potential to develop at any age. Yet the best proof of many her theories may well be the woman herself, possibly UC Berkeley's oldest lecturer and certainly one of its most popular.

"She was the most incredible teacher I ever had," said Robert Sproul, a 2003 graduate and orthopedic surgeon at UCSF. "To Dr. Diamond, anatomy is something that is alive, something she is passionate about and her students can't help getting excited about it too."

Being in the classroom with young people is "the best thing in the whole wide world," said Diamond, a recipient of UC Berkeley's Distinguished Teaching Award in 1975. Her office is covered with photos of former students. She remembers all of their names and the careers they pursued after graduation.

A UC Berkeley veteran

Except for several years spent at Harvard and Cornell universities early in her career, Diamond has been at UC Berkeley since the mid-1940s, when she was an undergraduate studying biology and playing on the tennis team. Diamond's parents and her four children from a previous marriage also attended UC Berkeley.

She is so committed to the campus that when she married a UCLA neuroscience professor almost 30 years ago, they began a commuting marriage. Her husband, Arnold Scheibel, is still teaching a graduate course and lives in Los Angeles. They see each other most weekends and, though they had to give up golf, they enjoy playing pingpong.

Spending the weekdays apart "gives a certain flavor to our relationship," said Scheibel, 87. "It adds a lot of piquancy to it."

Scheibel said his wife's ideas often have been ahead of their time and her outspoken nature has made her somewhat of a lightning rod in the male-dominated world of science.

"She is a very unusual woman who thinks very creatively, outside the box," he said. "That has not always been appreciated."

Overcoming gender

Diamond began her graduate work in 1948 and was the first female student in the department of anatomy. The first task she was given was to sew a cover for a large magnifying machine.

"They didn't know what to do with me because they weren't used to having a woman," Diamond said. "They thought I was there to get a husband. I was there to learn."

Such challenges were not uncommon. Years later she requested tissue samples of Albert Einstein's brain from a pathologist in Missouri. He didn't trust her.

"He wasn't sure that I was a scientist. This is one thing that you have to face being a woman," said Diamond, who persisted for three years before she received four sugar-cube-sized pieces of the physicist's brain. "He didn't think that I should be the one to be looking at Einstein's brain."

Diamond became one of only a few researchers to analyze slices of Einstein's cortex. She found that Einstein had twice as many glial cells as normal males, a 1985 discovery that caused an international sensation as well as scientific skepticism.

Scientists previously believed neurons were responsible for thinking and glial cells were support cells. Researchers now believe that glial cells play a critical role in brain development, learning, memory, aging and disease.

Trailblazing work

Diamond has spent decades in the laboratory researching how environmental factors can alter the anatomy of the brain. She found that rats living in cages with stimulating objects and challenging activities developed dramatically thicker cerebral cortices than rats living without such stimulation.

"I think scientifically she is one of the key people who opened up the field of thought that the brain is malleable in response to environmental exposure," said Dr. Robert Knight, director of UC Berkeley's Helen Wills Neuroscience Institute. "Now that work is all the rage."

Not content to let her ideas sit on some library shelf, she traveled to Cambodia in 2001 to work with orphans living in a forested Buddhist compound with no electricity or running water. Her goals were to improve their nutrition, teach them English and computer skills, and give them love.

She raised $65,000 for the project and returned five more times. Three of the children are now in college.

Early foundation

Diamond was raised on 20 acres of orchards and vegetable gardens in the foothills north of Los Angeles.

She was the youngest of six brothers and sisters with whom she played badminton, swam in the pool and ate pomegranates on the stone wall surrounding the property. Their chores included pruning the fruit trees, milking the goats and driving tractors. "That's how we all became worker bees," she said.

Diamond said she was given a lot of freedom and would spend days hiking in the mountains with her dog.

Her father was a physician who moved to California from northern England, a "strict but kind" man who intimidated the rest of the children, but not Diamond. She said he taught her to be unafraid of strong, dominant men - a characteristic that served her well in her professional life.

Another source of inspiration were the accomplished artists who were patients of her father in the tight-knit community of La Crescenta (Los Angeles County). One of them, Disney animator Ben Sharpsteen, once told her: "Someday you're going to amount to something."

Artistic touch

That early artistic environment also motivated Diamond to learn to paint. The dining room of her north Berkeley home is decorated with Impressionist-style oil paintings she did on a trip to Monet's Garden in France and pastel portraits she drew of her twin grandsons.

It was a drawing that stoked Diamond's fascination with the brain. As a 17-year-old college freshman, she saw an illustration of a head and neck in the textbook "Gray's Anatomy."

"It combines aesthetics with anatomy," Diamond said of the drawing that now hangs on her office door. "Its elegant simplicity won my heart."

Diamond received her doctorate the same month she gave birth to her first child in 1953. She was married for 30 years to Richard Diamond, a nuclear chemist at the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory, and the couple had four children.

Her youngest daughter, Ann Diamond, grew up going to her mother's lab and playing in the creek next to the Valley Life Sciences Building.

"The university was my playground," said Ann Diamond, now a physician in Washington. She calls her mother a "graceful athlete" who was still doing handstands off the high dive well into her 60s.

It is clear that she still has abundant energy.

Knight, the Cal neuroscience institute chief, remembered an annual graduate student picnic a few years ago.

Diamond showed up "wearing a white blouse, white pants and her ever-present scarf," he said. "She played softball and when she was done, there wasn't a mark on her."

Brain's link to immunity

Diamond found another secret to successful aging a decade ago when she established a link between the brain and the immune system.

She invited members of the Orinda women's bridge club to the lab and took blood samples from the elderly women before and after they played the card game. Diamond found that the mental challenge of bridge significantly increased their disease-fighting white blood cells. Meanwhile, a control group of women who sat listening to quiet music while the others were playing bridge did not show a boost in their immunity.

Although she has cut back on her class schedule, Diamond plans to continue teaching as long as she remains healthy. In fact, one of the highlights of her year is when 15 former students, including Sproul, return to campus in the fall to share their medical expertise with her applied anatomy class. Sproul said he enjoys spending time with Diamond because he always leaves her feeling more positive about his own life.

"She makes me want to be a better doctor," he said.
